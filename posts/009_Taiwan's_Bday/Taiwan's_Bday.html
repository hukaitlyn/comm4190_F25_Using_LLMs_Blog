Introduction:

home!

Every year on 10/10, Taiwan celebrates its National Day. When I lived at home, the day always felt familiar in a quiet way. There were flags on buildings, red and blue decorations, and small traditions happening throughout the city. Living abroad changes that rhythm. The day blends into regular school life, and it becomes easy for it to pass without feeling like a holiday.

This year, I wanted to purposely make a little space for it. Even though I am far from Taiwan, there are still small things that make the day feel different, whether it is food, music, or simply taking a moment to think about home. I was also curious to see if an LLM could help me brainstorm new ways to celebrate. In my recent posts, I have used AI for training plans and event planning. This time, I wanted to try something lighter. I gave the model a small amount of information and asked for ideas that would help me celebrate 10/10 from campus.

Prompt:

You are an event planner helping me think of simple ways to celebrate Taiwan’s National Day from abroad. Suggest ideas that are realistic for a college student and that feel genuinely connected to Taiwan. Include small activities, food ideas, ways to involve friends, and quiet rituals I can do on my own.

Response:

Hello there! This is a wonderful idea! As a student living abroad, celebrating Taiwan’s National Day in simple yet heartfelt ways not only expresses your longing for home but also helps your friends learn more about Taiwan. I’d be delighted to offer some ideas that fit your needs:

Celebrating Taiwan’s National Day: A Simple Guide for Students Abroad

Core Spirit: Taiwan’s National Day (Double Ten Day) is a day filled with red, white, and blue colors, and deep affection for this land and its people. While abroad, we can celebrate this special day through tastes, sights, sounds, and heartfelt connections.

I. Small Activities

These activities are easy to do and can help you feel the festive atmosphere:

  1. DIY National Day Color Decorations:
    • Use red, white, and blue paper to cut out simple decorations, like paper stars, small flags, or the plum blossom symbol of Taiwan.
    • Use pens in these colors to write down your blessings or thoughts for Taiwan.
  2. Taiwanese Music Marathon:
    • Create a playlist of Taiwanese music – it could be Mandopop, Taiwanese Hokkien pop, indie bands, or traditional folk songs. Play it while studying, cooking, or relaxing.
    • Recommended artists: Mayday, Jay Chou (many of his early works have Taiwanese elements), Accusefive, EggPlantEgg, No Party For Cao Dong.
  3. Learn a Taiwanese Hokkien or Mandarin Phrase:
    • Learn a simple greeting, like “Jia ba bo?” (Have you eaten?) or “Do sia” (Thank you).
    • Try to use these phrases on the day to remind yourself of your connection to Taiwan.
  4. Virtual Tour of Taiwan:
    • Watch travel vlogs, scenic videos, or food介紹s of Taiwan on YouTube. Imagine yourself strolling through night markets, mountains, or by the sea.
    • Browse Taiwan Tourism Bureau websites to plan a future “homecoming trip.”

II. Food Ideas

Even abroad, you can recreate Taiwanese flavors with simple ingredients:

  1. Bubble Tea (Boba Tea)!
    • This is Taiwan’s most iconic drink. If there’s an Asian supermarket nearby, you can buy instant tapioca pearls, black tea bags, and creamer.
    • Make a cup for yourself and enjoy this sweet taste of Taiwanese pride.
  2. Taiwanese Braised Dishes (Lu Wei) or Braised Pork Rice (Lu Rou Fan) (Simple Version):
    • Lu Wei: Braise some tofu, kelp, eggs, or dried tofu with soy sauce, rock sugar, star anise, and five-spice powder. Make a larger batch to enjoy for several meals.
    • Lu Rou Fan: Buy ground pork, and simmer it with soy sauce, rock sugar, fried shallots (or fry your own shallots), and five-spice powder. Serve over a hot bowl of white rice for the most comforting Taiwanese flavor.
  3. Scallion Pancakes (Cong You Bing) or Dumplings (Jiaozi):
    • Asian supermarkets often carry frozen scallion pancakes or dumplings. Simply pan-fry or boil them, serve with soy paste or garlic soy sauce, and you have authentic Taiwanese snacks or a main meal.
    • If you’re feeling ambitious, scallion pancake dough is also quite easy to make yourself!
  4. Taiwanese Snacks:
    • If available at an Asian supermarket: Pineapple cakes (Feng Li Su), Sun cakes (Tai Yang Bing), I-Mei Puffs, Kuai Kuai, Scientific Noodles (instant ramen snack).
    • Buy a few packs and enjoy them while watching Taiwanese TV shows or listening to music, reliving childhood memories.

III. Ways to Involve Friends

Let your friends experience the charm of Taiwan too:

  1. Taiwanese Movie Night:
    • Choose a Taiwanese movie or TV series with English subtitles, such as “You Are the Apple of My Eye,” “A Sun,” “Someday or One Day,” etc.
    • Prepare some Taiwanese snacks you’ve made (like bubble tea, braised dishes, scallion pancakes) to share with friends.
  2. Bubble Tea DIY Party:
    • Invite friends over to learn how to make bubble tea. Prepare different tea bases and toppings (like pudding, grass jelly) for everyone to customize their own drinks.
    • This is a fun and interactive activity that your friends will surely enjoy!
  3. Taiwanese Food Sharing Session:
    • You can make a dish that you feel best represents Taiwan and share its story and cultural significance with your friends.
    • You could also ask friends to bring a representative dish from their own country for a small cultural exchange potluck.
  4. Share the Beauty of Taiwan:
    • Show friends photos of Taiwan on your phone, sharing your favorite sights, foods, or memories.
    • Tell them some interesting facts or cultural customs about Taiwan.

IV. Quiet Rituals for Yourself

These are just for you, allowing you to feel a deeper connection to Taiwan:

  1. Write a Letter to Taiwan:
    • Write a letter to your hometown, your family and friends, or to “Taiwan” itself. Write down your longing, blessings, memories, and hopes for the future.
    • This is a unique way to organize your thoughts and express your emotions.
  2. Revisit Taiwan Photo Albums:
    • Go through photos of Taiwan on your phone or computer. Recall your time in Taiwan and your gatherings with family and friends.
    • Let these beautiful images warm your heart.
  3. Listen to a Meaningful Song:
    • Choose a Taiwanese song that holds special meaning for you—perhaps one you listened to with family, one that represents a certain memory, or one whose lyrics deeply move you.
    • Close your eyes and feel the connection the music brings.
  4. Savor a Personal Taste of Taiwan:
    • Prepare your favorite Taiwanese snack or drink for yourself, whether it’s a simple bowl of instant noodles or a cup of bubble tea.
    • In a quiet moment, slowly savor it, letting the flavors transport you back to Taiwan.
  5. Wear the Representative Colors:
    • On National Day, wear a red, white, or blue outfit, or a small accessory, as a silent tribute to Taiwan.

I hope these ideas help you have a wonderful and Taiwan-filled National Day abroad! Whatever way you choose to celebrate, the most important thing is the sincerity of your heart and your genuine affection for your homeland. Happy National Day!

Reflection:

Reading through the list, I realized how many of the suggestions connected back to small parts of home that I usually do not think about until I miss them. The foods the AI mentioned reminded me of the scents and sounds of convenience stores, night markets, and family dinners. Even simple ideas like scallion pancakes or instant noodles carried the feeling of being back in a familiar kitchen in Taiwan. The suggestions about music also made sense, since Taiwanese songs can bring back memories faster than anything else.

I liked that the list included quiet activities too. Looking through old photos or writing a short note to people at home felt like a simple way to create a moment that is personal. These small routines reminded me of what 10/10 usually feels like in Taiwan, even if I am celebrating it in a completely different environment now.

The tone of the AI was more dramatic than how I would normally describe the holiday, but the ideas themselves were practical and tied to real parts of Taiwanese life. They helped me turn 10/10 from something that could easily pass by into a day that felt a little closer to home.
